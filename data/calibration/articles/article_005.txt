Tova O’Brien is Stuff’s Chief Political Correspondent and host of the political podcast, Tova. ANALYSIS: As well as marking the annual International Day of Awesomeness, this year, March 10th will also be the day Chlöe Swarbrick is announced as the new co-leader of the Green Party. If - following some unexpected tear in the space-time continuum - Swarbrick and the party fail to pull off this inevitability it will create another inevitability: the end of their steady ascension in the polls since Shaw took over the co-leadership in 2015. Swarbrick is fiercely intelligent, popular, passionate, has an enormous profile and stellar self-proclaimed ‘woke’ credentials befitting the Green co-leadership. The dream scenario being willed by cooler heads within the party is a single Swarbrick ticket; the best and most unifying person for the job running unopposed. It would ensure a steady, stable transition of power, helping the Greens get back on track after a turbulent start to the year with the Golriz Ghahraman saga . That’s the obvious, most sensible thing to do. But the Greens are many things, predictable they are not. The stability of the party wasn’t helped by a clumsy outing from the remaining co-leader during Shaw’s big resignation news conference on Tuesday. Marama Davidson repeatedly failed to shoot down questions about whether she too would be stepping down, “I have learned that I take things day by day and right now I am here and committed.” Stuff understands that behind the scenes Davidson has only ever expressed her intention to stay on and lead the party into the 2026 election. Why she couldn’t just say that publicly was both baffling and unsettling for the party. Compounding that disconcertment are the myriad variables thrown up by the Greens’ arguably overly democratic party processes. From Wednesday when nominations open the party embarks on something akin to - and as convoluted as - the presidential primaries currently underway in the United States. Members attend local meetings to vote on allocating delegates. The more members a local branch has, the more delegates they get. Any member of the party - not just MPs but any member - can put themselves forward to lead the party. This is how Dunedin software developer and climate activist James Cockle was able to stage a coup of sorts against Shaw in 2021, challenging him for the co-leadership at the party’s annual general meeting where the leaders are re-elected each year. Shaw won by a landslide. It’s also how a former Green MP like Gareth Hughes could potentially - though extremely unlikely - have a crack at the leadership from outside parliament. Nuts but feasible. The candidates can be any gender thanks to a change in 2022 which shifted the co-leadership model from ‘one man, one woman’ to one woman plus one leader of any gender - and one of the leaders must be Māori. There’s a bunch of Freemason-esque pledge stuff the nominees have to do, promising not to compromise Green values or bring the party into disrepute. An easy tick for her royal-righteousness, Chloe Swarbrick. Although unlikely Swarbrick could potentially face a challenge from caucus colleague Teanau Tuiono. Tuiono became the Greens first Pasifika MP when he was elected on the list in 2020. He also dilly dallied a little too long for some in the Greens when Shaw faced yet another leadership challenge in 2022 (this time he was actually briefly ousted) and Tuiono called a random news conference to declare, "I'm not ruling anything in, not ruling anything out, I'm just kind of giving it a think.” Greens 4IC Julie Anne Genter also left the door ajar during that contest but that could have been out of a fear that if Shaw bailed out completely at the time, there would be no one to fill the void. Now that Genter has ‘done a Swarbrick’ and won an electorate seat against the odds and against the polls - taking Rongotai like Swarbrick did Auckland Central - that’s likely to be her greater focus. List MP Efeso Collins is the other unlikely wildcard. Unlikely because the former Auckland mayoral hopeful is both a newbie to the parliament, coming in with the 2023 intake, and a newbie to the Greens, jumping ship from Labour to run in ‘23. Besides, in its heart of hearts the Greens know they should get behind one candidate and one candidate only - Swarbrick. That said, even if Swarbrick is the only candidate she’ll still need to go through the full agonising appointment process. The party’s 39 page constitution allows members to “reopen nominations” with 25% support. So to actually be elected Swarbrick will need at least 75% of delegate votes. That will perhaps be easier now than when Elizabeth Kerekere was still an MP. She quit parliament soon after famously calling Swarbrick a crybaby and Green sources say she was the agitator-in-chief against Swarbrick, something Kerekere has denied. Given Davidson’s wishy washy, speculation-inducing failure to rule out quitting herself, there’s a very high chance that any of those ambitious wannabe leaders will get another crack in a few years' time when Davidson actually stands down. The pool then, however, will be restricted to Māori candidates only. Former Greens’ Chief of Staff now Wellington Mayor, Tory Whanau, is another name that’s cropped up as a future leader, though she would have to renew her party membership first. Bearing all of this in mind - the process, the potential alternatives, the need for a smooth and stable transition - there really can be only one. Shaw says he’s refusing to publicly or privately endorse any MP or candidate but it would be a final impressive notch to cap off an already impressive legacy (note: he says questions of legacy make him queasy) to ensure a successful succession to Swarbrick.
