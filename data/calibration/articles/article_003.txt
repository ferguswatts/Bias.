OPINION: “I’m doing my best.” So said David Seymour of his failing school lunch programme last week. Well, he is trying, bless him. Seymour is undeniably one of the sharpest Opposition politicians of his generation. With a formidable intellect and a talent for grabbing headlines, the ACT leader revived his party from obscurity to government over a decade. Opposition, he’s great at. Governing? Not so much. Soon to be deputy prime minister, the school lunches fiasco has underlined that Seymour is struggling to adapt from politicking to political office. Last week, the associate education minister couldn’t explain how he came to take charge of Ka Ora, Ka Ako, a programme he had previously criticised for wasteful spending. From Margaret Thatcher ‘milk snatcher’ to Jamie Oliver’s war on turkey twizzlers in British schools and footballer Marcus Rashford scoring against Boris Johnson on free meals, politicians rarely win a food fight over school dinners. Taking responsibility for the dining choices of eight-year-olds alone demonstrates a naivety about the realities of governing. Seymour’s vision for feeding the nation’s children is, of course, efficiency-driven, market-minded, and unbothered by the idea that kids might actually need to enjoy the food they’re given. Under his school lunch reform, the solution is: fewer lunches, fewer costs, more responsibility shifted onto parents. The free market will fix it. And if it doesn’t: Let them eat Marmite sandwiches ! Dickensian baddie is a tricky optic to pull off. Especially when coupled with flat-footed political management. The debacle started with bombast. Seymour promised to bring the cost of each meal down to $3, saving taxpayers over $130 million a year. This he could do without compromising on quality or nutrition, he quixotically insisted. Predictably, since it launched, the government has endured almost daily headlines about late or inedible meals. Teachers, not traditionally sympathetic to National-led governments, have relished telling tales out of school. Libelle, one of the providers, has now collapsed, owing creditors millions , including 60 schools. Its liquidation has raised questions about whether $3 was ever do-able. All of this was predictable. Certainly, officials did: warning last year that the cut-price contract could push Libelle into making commitments which it could not deliver. The fuzzy maths behind the impulses of right wing populism was laid bare. Leftover lunches were thrown out or fed to a pig called Crackles and schools spent extra to buy replacement food for hungry kids. Defying all the usual rules of how to handle a political scandal, Seymour has let this pan out into a fully fledged disaster. When questioned, he is rigid and defensive, with the demeanour of a man forced to eat a woke quinoa salad when he ordered a steak. Accusing critics of nitpicking and moaning about media coverage is a strategy, up to a point. That point is when children start to be maimed by exploding cottage pies and melting plastic. Melissa Lee, Penny Simmonds and Matt Doocey were all demoted for less. Seymour hangs on because Prime Minister Christopher Luxon has a pathological aversion to bossing his coalition partners. Rather than rolling up his sleeves and using recess week to fix the fiasco, Seymour dodged the heat and got out of the kitchen. Straight from the frying pan and into the fire of local government. Brandishing a traffic cone, Seymour staged a road-side press conference to announce ACT will run candidates in this year’s local elections. At just the time when ACT should be focusing on shoring up its parliamentary strength and momentum into 2026, the party will pull focus and spread itself thin across multiple local races. Local elections require a lot of expense and effort — campaigning, advertising, debates, and policy proposals — but the rewards are limited. And ACT already lacks grassroots representation. Local politics also attracts lunatics — and ACT had enough bother in 2023 weeding conspiracy theorists from its general election candidate line-up. And barring Wellington, council voters dislike partisan politics in local decision-making. Seymour says he wants “a clean out” of local bodies. If he was genuinely interested in change — and not just taking advantage of the parliamentary recess news vacuum — his party would be championing desperately needed local-government reform at a national level. For all his posturing, Seymour’s time in government has been less a triumph of libertarian ideals and more a master class in the dangers of an attention economy. Even in the policy area where ACT was expected to drive transformation — reforming the machinery of government — he has fallen short. His case for reinvention was to create another new ministry , stuffed with more highly paid bureaucrats. Populism thrives on outrage, and Seymour has expertly positioned himself as the man who will always have an inflammatory quote ready for whatever the news cycle demands. But when it comes to actual governance? Less so. His populism collides uncomfortably with the grim realities of running a country where not everything can be solved with a glib sound bite or a dorky TikTok. The problem with selling yourself as the anti-politician is that eventually, people start expecting results rather than just quips. He’s doing his best. It’s just not good enough for office.
